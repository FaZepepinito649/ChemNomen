    Substituent Prefixes
    When a group is not the principal group, it attaches as a prefix — listed alphabetically
    GroupPrefix usedExample name
    –CH₃ (methyl group)methyl-2-methylpropane
    –C₂H₅ (ethyl group)ethyl-3-ethylhexane
    –C₃H₇ (propyl group)propyl-4-propyloctane
    –Ffluoro-2-fluorobutane
    –Clchloro-chloroethane, 2-chloropropane
    –Brbromo-2-bromopropane
    –Iiodo-1-iodopropane
    –COOH (when not principal)carboxy-3-carboxypropanal — aldehyde is principal, –COOH is substituent
    –CHO (when not principal)formyl-4-formylbutanoic acid — acid is principal, –CHO is substituent
    –COOR (ester, when not principal)alkoxycarbonyl- e.g. methoxycarbonyl-3-(methoxycarbonyl)propanoic acid — acid is principal, –COOCH₃ is substituent
    –CONH₂ (amide, when not principal)carbamoyl-3-carbamoylpropanoic acid — acid is principal, –CONH₂ is substituent
    –OH (when not principal)hydroxy-3-hydroxypentanal
    –NH₂ (when not principal)amino-3-aminopropanoic acid
    C=O ketone (when not principal)oxo-4-oxopentanal — aldehyde is principal, ketone C=O is substituent
    –OCH₃methoxy-2-methoxypropane
    –OC₂H₅ethoxy-1-ethoxybutane
    –NO₂nitro-nitrobenzene, 1-nitropropane
    –C₆H₅ (phenyl)phenyl-2-phenylbutane
    📌 Alphabetical order rule: list all substituents alphabetically by their prefix name, ignoring di/tri/tetra multipliers. Bromo comes before chloro before ethyl before fluoro before methyl.

    💡 Why carboxy- and not -oic acid? Because only one group can be the suffix. If your molecule has both –COOH and –CHO, aldehyde loses (lower priority) and becomes formyl-; the acid wins and sets the suffix -oic acid.

    Aromatic Position Prefixes — o / m / p
    Shorthand for the position of two substituents on a benzene ring
    PrefixFull namePositionsGeometryExample
    o-ortho1,2adjacent carbonso-xylene = 1,2-dimethylbenzene
    m-meta1,3one carbon apartm-dichlorobenzene = 1,3-dichlorobenzene
    p-para1,4opposite sidesp-xylene = 1,4-dimethylbenzene
    💡 o/m/p only apply to disubstituted benzene. For 3+ substituents always use locant numbers (1,2,3-…). These labels are common in industry but IUPAC prefers the numbered system.
    🏆
    Priority Order — Which Group Gets the Suffix?
    The highest-priority group present sets the suffix; all others become prefixes
    →
    Step 1: Find all functional groups in the molecule. Pick the one highest on the ladder — that gives the suffix.
    →
    Step 2: All other functional groups and substituents become prefixes (hydroxy-, amino-, oxo-, bromo-…).
    →
    Step 3: Choose the parent chain as the longest chain that includes the principal group carbon.
    →
    Step 4: Number the chain to give the principal group the lowest possible locant.
    →
    Step 5: List all substituent prefixes alphabetically (ignore di/tri when sorting) before the parent name.

    Part 4 — Stoichiometry (5 pts)
    Combustión de butano — mismo formato que el examen de ethane
    BALANCED EQUATION:
    2 C₄H₁₀ + 13 O₂ → 8 CO₂ + 10 H₂O
    MoleculeM (g/mol)Role
    C₄H₁₀ (butane)58 g/molReactant — 4×12 + 10×1
    O₂32 g/molReactant
    CO₂44 g/molProduct
    H₂O18 g/molProduct
    3 pasos — siempre los mismos:
    1
    g → mol: mol = gramos ÷ M (peso molecular)
    2
    mol reactivo → mol producto: usar la razón molar de la ecuación balanceada
    3
    mol → g: gramos = mol × M
    📝 Ejemplo: 116 g C₄H₁₀ → mol = 116÷58 = 2 mol → mol CO₂ = 2×(8÷2) = 8 mol → g CO₂ = 8×44 = 352 g

    Part 5 — Reactions
    Las 5 que salieron: Fermentation · Hydrogenation · Halogenation · Neutralization · Oxidation
    🍺 Fermentation
    Reactants
    Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)
    Products
    Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) + CO₂
    Catalyst
    Enzymes (from yeast) — biological catalyst
    Conditions
    Anaerobic (no O₂), ~37°C
    C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2 C₂H₅OH + 2 CO₂
    🔧 Hydrogenation
    Reactants
    Alkene + H₂
    Products
    Alkane (saturated)
    Catalyst
    Ni, Pt, or Pd (metal catalyst)
    Conditions
    ~150°C, metal surface
    C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆
    ☀️ Halogenation of Alkanes (Substitution)
    Reactants
    Alkane + Cl₂ (or Br₂)
    Products
    Haloalkane + HCl
    Catalyst
    UV light (hν) — free radical initiation
    Conditions
    UV radiation required (NOT Lewis acid)
    CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl
    ⚖️ Neutralization (Carboxylic Acid + Base)
    Reactants
    Carboxylic acid (R–COOH) + NaOH
    Products
    Carboxylate salt (R–COONa) + H₂O
    Catalyst
    None
    Conditions
    Aqueous solution, room temperature
    CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O
    🔴 Complete Oxidation of a Primary Alcohol
    Reactants
    Primary alcohol (R–CH₂OH)
    Products
    Carboxylic acid (via aldehyde intermediate)
    Catalyst
    KMnO₄ or K₂Cr₂O₇ (acidic conditions)
    Conditions
    Acid (H₂SO₄), reflux / KMnO₄ purple→colorless / K₂Cr₂O₇ orange→green
    CH₃CH₂OH →[O]→ CH₃CHO →[O]→ CH₃COOH
    ⚠️ Secondary → ketone only (stops there). Tertiary → NO reaction (no H on C bearing –OH)
